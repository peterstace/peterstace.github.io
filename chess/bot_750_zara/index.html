Game Analysis: Zara (Bot 750)

2021-02-07, Peter Stace

Score sheet

  1. d4 b5
  2. Bf4 e5
  3. Bxe5 Ba6
  4. Nf3 h5
  5. e3 Rh6
  6. Bf4 Be7
  7. Bxh6 gxh6
  8. Bd3 Kf8
  9. O-O Bb4
  10. c3 Ba5
  11. b4 h4
  12. bxa5 Ke8
  13. Ne5 Ne7
  14. Qh5 Qc8
  15. Nxf7 Nbc6
  16. Nd6+ Kd8
  17. Qe8#

Analysis

  1. d4

White intends the London system.

  1. …b5

Black uses the Polish defence.

  1. Bf4

Dark squared Bishop is developed as per the London system.

  1. … e5

Black hangs the King’s pawn. It’s attacked twice but is undefended.

  1. Bxe5

White captures the undefended king’s pawn using the bishop. The alternative was to capture using the pawn. This was decided against because it would break the London opening pawn structure.

  1. … Ba6

It’s not clear why Black played this move. The b-pawn is now defended, however the bishop cannot see its main a6-f1 diagonal because it’s blocked by the b-pawn.

  1. Nf3

White develops the knight as per the London system.

  1. … h5

Black pushed their h-pawn to allow future activation of their king-side rook.

  1. e3

White pushes the e-pawn into its London system position, and adds a third defender to the d-pawn (the queen and knight already defend). The engine suggests e4, Nbd2, or a2 as stronger moves. I didn’t consider e4 since I wanted to stay in the London system. And Nbd2 would block in the queen. And a2 doesn’t seem appealing.

  1. … Rh6

Black activates their rook.

  1. Bf4

White attacks the rook. The engine says this is the best move.

  1. … Be7

Black actives their bishop but without an attack or threat, leaving their rook underdefended against White’s bishop. The rook is defended by the knight and g-pawn. However, the attacking bishop is worth less than the bishop.

  1. Bxh6

White takes the underdefended rook.

  1. … gxh6

Black takes back with the g-pawn. This leaves them with doubled pawns on the h file. If Black had instead taken back with the knight, then they would have kept a better pawn structure, albeit with a knight on the edge of the board.

  1. Bd3

White actives their light bishop as per the London system.

  1. … Kf8

Black moves their king, giving up the right to castle. It’s not immediately obvious to me why Black did this. The engine states that this is the best move, although I’m not sure why.

  1. O-O

White castles because development is almost complete. The only minor piece left to develop is the queen-side knight.

  1. … Bb4

Black further develops their dark bishop (the second time it has moved). It may have been better for them to move the bishop to b4 initially.

  1. c3

White pushes the c-pawn to put pressure on the dark squared bishop. The bishop cannot take c3, since it is defended by the b-pawn. And it cannot move to a3, since that would be attacked by the b-pawn. So it is forced to retreat, gaining White a tempo.

  1. … Ba5

Black retreats the bishop to a5 in order to avoid losing it. It is now trapped with very few places to go. It may have been better for Black to retreat to d6 or e7, because White can play b4 winning the bishop.

  1. b4

White attacks the bishop and is defended by the c-pawn. This forces the bishop to retreat again.

  1. … h4

Black must not have seen the immediate threat on the bishop and does not respond to it.

  1. bxa5

White carries out the threat on the dark squared bishop.

  1. … Ke8

It’s not clear why Black made this move. The engine suggests h3 instead, which would have threatened to remove the g-pawn in front of White’s king (either because White would have taken using that pawn, or black would have taken that pawn directly, threatening White’s rook on f1).

  1. Ne5

White places their knight in the center of the board. The engine suggests this as the best move.

  1. … Ne7

Black moves the knight to occupy the square in front of the king, although it’s not attacking anything.

  1. Qh5

White attacks f7 both with the queen and the knight. White has 2 attackers, and the king is the only defender.

  1. … Qc8

The engine states that this is a major blunder. Black should have played Ng6 in order to block White’s queen from threatening mate on f7.

  1. Nxf7

White missed mate in 1. They should have attacked f7 using the queen instead, which would have forced the king to retreat to d8 ending the game on the next move with Qf8#.

  1. … Nbc6

Black develops their knight, possibly with the intention of rerouting it to attack White’s knight.

  1. Nd6+

White performs a double check. The knight checks the king from its new location, and also reveals a discovered attack by the queen. Furthermore, the knight is also attacking Black’s queen.

  1. … Kd8

Black only had 2 legal moves, both of which allow mate in 1. If black has played Kf8, then White could have responded with Qf7#.

  1. Qe8#

White wins.
